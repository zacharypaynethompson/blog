I'm at the Cock Tavern on Mare Street,[^1] halfway through a Marmite pickled egg,[^2] when a friend pulls up my last blog post on his phone and says "I like how you implied the only reason people don't eat British food every day is because it makes them sleepy."

Which is a fair pisstake. I'd written that traditional British food is heavy, that "if you eat that every night, and you work a desk job, you're gonna feel... lethargic."[^3] But it's not just that. A lot of British food is quite bland too, if you don't have good quality ingredients and don't make it well, which often you don't. People like flavour. Everyone's always going on about spices.

"That's the Protestant work ethic," someone says, and the table nods like this explains something. This is an ongoing bit that some of my friends have, where the Protestant work ethic gets invoked to explain basically anything about British life, as if Weber's *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905) is the unified field theory of why we are the way we are. It's just shorthand for grindset, for staying late, for not taking lunch. Probably the most cited book that nobody at the table has read.

Then someone brings up the Italians, doing a cliché accent. "Giuseppe has to stare at the pot for eighteen hours. He has to make-a sure the ragù, it never boils. He stirs, and he tastes, and he waits." And that's why they don't get anything done. Meanwhile the British guy is grabbing an eel out of the Thames and shoving it in his mouth because he's got canals to build and there's no time for this.

Obviously Italian food is delicious precisely *because* someone is staring at the pot. But there is a real question underneath: why *is* British food like that? Not just heavy, but fast, portable, designed for function over pleasure. Pie and mash. Fish and chips. Cornish pasties. Dense flour-based puddings packed with sugar. When did it all become so focused on just getting calories in, and when did it stop being spiced?

## british food used to be heavily spiced

This is the part most people don't know. Medieval English cooking was full of spice. *The Forme of Cury*, compiled around 1390 by Richard II's cooks, uses ginger, saffron, cinnamon, cloves and pepper throughout.[^4] Christmas pudding's ingredient list, nutmeg, cinnamon, cloves, allspice, is basically a fossil of this tradition, a medieval spice profile preserved in a single dish while the rest of the cuisine moved on without it.

Spices were expensive because the supply chain was absurd. Pepper from India's Malabar Coast, cloves and nutmeg from the Maluku Islands in modern Indonesia, passing through something like twelve intermediaries, local producers, Indian Ocean traders, Arab merchants, and finally Venetian buyers who held near-exclusive European purchasing rights.[^5] A pound of pepper in 14th-century England cost one to two days' wages for a skilled labourer.[^6] The term "peppercorn rent" comes from the fact that peppercorns were genuinely valuable enough to function as payment.

This is partly what drove colonial expansion. The East India Company was chartered on 31 December 1600 specifically to break into the spice trade and cut out the middlemen.[^7] The whole project was, at least initially, about securing cheaper access to the flavours that English cooking already used. Britain colonised half the world in part because it wanted its food to taste good.

## so where did the spices go

The spices didn't disappear because of some theological prohibition against flavour. They disappeared because they got cheap.

Once the Portuguese and Dutch established direct trade routes, prices fell, and when spices stopped being expensive they stopped being status symbols. The upper classes dropped them. By the late 16th and 17th centuries, new culinary philosophies were emphasising the "natural" flavours of ingredients over heavy spicing.[^8]

Sugar went the other way. Colonialism gave Britain cheap spices and cheap sugar, and the cuisine absorbed them very differently. Sugar stayed expensive and entrenched itself as a status marker well into the 18th century, underwritten by the Atlantic slave economy.[^9] This is why Christmas pudding kept its medieval spice profile: the spices survived in the one context where they still read as luxury, wrapped in sugar. And it's why British puddings got so aggressively sweet while savoury cooking went plain. The sweetness and the blandness aren't separate stories, they're the same class dynamics playing out on different ingredients.

France built a whole replacement system. La Varenne's *Le Cuisinier François* in 1651 is basically the founding document of modern French cooking, moving explicitly away from medieval spice-heavy sauces toward butter, herbs and stock.[^10] This is where the French cuisine we recognise starts. It was a deliberate, sophisticated pivot from one flavour system to another.

Britain didn't do that. Or rather, the rich did: they imported French cuisine wholesale. Escoffier at the Savoy, French-trained chefs in country houses, Mrs Beeton sitting somewhere in the middle translating it for the aspirational middle class.[^11] Britain got a replacement cuisine for people who could afford it. Everyone else got pies.

The Reformation made the gap worse. Puritanism didn't produce a specific ban on tasty food, but it created a cultural atmosphere suspicious of sensory indulgence in general. William Prynne attacked feasting alongside theatre as ungodly excess in 1633.[^12] English cooking became deliberately austere after the Reformation, in contrast to its elaborate medieval predecessor.[^13] If taking pleasure in food is borderline sinful, you're probably not going to invest much effort in developing a replacement for the spices you just lost.

This wasn't uniquely British, either. German and Dutch food get characterised as bland in exactly the same way, heavy, starch-forward, sausage-and-potato. The same confluence of forces, Protestantism, industrialisation, the loss of a spice-using tradition without a distinctive replacement, produced similar outcomes across Northern Europe. Italy and France are the outliers, not Britain.

## then industrialisation locked it in

By the time the factories arrived, the food infrastructure had already been hollowed out. The enclosure movements of the 18th and early 19th centuries had stripped rural labourers of common land, which meant losing the domestic food economy that went with it: home-brewed small beer, kept pigs, garden vegetables.[^14] Cobbett's complaint about tea replacing beer sits inside this bigger displacement. The factory worker hadn't just had their time compressed. They'd had their whole food supply taken away and replaced with whatever could be bought cheaply.

The Factory Act 1833 was the first effective legislation restricting working hours and mandating meal breaks in textile mills.[^15] Before this, factory workers ate when they could, often snatching food between shifts in appalling conditions. The Mines Act of 1842 documented the nutrition of workers underground and it wasn't good. Subsequent Factory Acts in 1844, 1847 and 1850 progressively regulated break times across industries, but the breaks were short. If you've got thirty minutes to eat before your next shift, the food has to be quick, portable and calorie-dense.

This is where the food we think of as "traditionally British" actually comes from. Fish and chips emerged in the 1860s, cheap trawled cod and fried potatoes eaten from newspaper with your hands, no cutlery, no plate, no lingering.[^16] Pie and mash shops followed in the 1870s. The Cornish pasty became the definitive miners' lunch, a complete meal sealed in a thick pastry case that kept it warm for hours underground. The crimped edge supposedly served as a handle so tin miners with arsenic on their hands could hold it without contaminating the filling, then throw the crust away.[^17] Some pasties even had a savoury filling at one end and fruit at the other, a two-course meal you could eat with one hand.

Even the drinks tell the same story. Cobbett railed against the spread of tea among working people, arguing it was making them weak and pale compared to their beer-drinking forebears.[^18] He had a point, in a narrow sense: a pint of small beer provided 150 to 200 calories plus B vitamins from the yeast, while tea provided nothing unless you could afford milk and sugar. Workers had traditionally received beer rations as part of their wages. Tea was essentially zero-calorie, and for the poorest, that mattered. Even the national drink was, for a time, evaluated primarily on whether it could keep a labourer standing.

Everything pointed the same direction. The food got heavy, portable and fast. The spices were already gone. Puritanism had made plainness respectable. And the industrial economy needed workers fed and back on the floor, not lingering over a meal.

Then rationing finished the job. Food rationing ran from 1940 to 1954, fourteen years that hit exactly when British cooking might have started recovering.[^19] A generation of cooks grew up without access to ingredients. The skills and traditions that might have survived the industrial period had one more layer stripped away. By the time rationing ended, there wasn't much left to recover.

## none of this is inherent

Heavy and bland doesn't mean bad. A good pork pie is a good pork pie. Fish and chips done well is genuinely great. You just probably don't want to eat these exact dishes every single day, and the reason so much British food looks like this isn't because British people don't care about flavour. It's because a specific sequence of forces played out over about five hundred years: the spices got cheap so the rich stopped using them, sugar stayed expensive so the sweetness stayed, nobody built a replacement cuisine for ordinary people, Puritanism made plainness a virtue, enclosure stripped the rural food economy, industrialisation made speed a necessity, and rationing knocked out whatever was left.

That's history, not a national character trait.

Anyway, I'm still at the pub, and the Marmite pickled egg is nearly gone. It occurs to me that the egg isn't bland at all. It's deeply, aggressively flavoured in a way that would probably horrify anyone who isn't already committed to it. And maybe that's the more interesting thing about British food at its best. It's not bland, it's acquired-taste food: brown sauce, Marmite, real ale, extremely aged cheese, jellied eels if you're brave. A cuisine that rewards commitment rather than seducing on first contact. Which is, I think, a more honest description of what's going on than "Protestant work ethic," even if it's less fun to say at the pub.
