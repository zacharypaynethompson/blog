I'm at the Cock Tavern on Mare Street,[^1] halfway through a Marmite pickled egg,[^2] when a friend mentions my last blog saying - "I like how you implied the *only* reason people don't eat British food every day is because it makes them sleepy."

He's right to take the piss. I had written that traditional British food is heavy, that "if you eat that every night, and you work a desk job, you're gonna feel... lethargic."[^3] But it is also that a lot of British food is quite bland. That is if you don't have good quality ingredients and don't make it well, which often you don't. People like a variety of flavour and that's not something you'd turn to British cuisine for. But why is it like that?

"That's the Protestant work ethic," someone says, and the table nods like this explains something. This is an ongoing bit that some of my friends have, where the Protestant work ethic gets invoked to explain basically anything about British life, as if Weber's *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905) is the unified field theory of why we are the way we are. It's just shorthand for "grindset", for staying late, for not indulging in superficial things like lunch. 

Then someone brings up the Italians, doing a cliché accent. "Giuseppe has to stare at the pot for eighteen hours. He has to make-a sure the ragù, it never boils. He stirs, and he tastes, and he waits." And that's why they don't get anything done. Meanwhile the British guy is grabbing an eel out of the Thames and shoving it in his mouth because he's got canals to build and there's no time for this.

Obviously Italian food is delicious precisely *because* someone is taking such care of their food. But there is a real question underneath: why *is* British food like that? Not just heavy, but fast, portable, designed for function over pleasure. Pie and mash. Fish and chips. Cornish pasties. Dense flour-based puddings packed with sugar. And beyond the heaviness, it is also quite bland, which is strange when you think about it, because didn't Britain colonise half the world precisely to get access to spices?

## british food used to be heavily spiced

Medieval English cooking was, actually, full of spice. *The Forme of Cury*, compiled around 1390 by Richard II's cooks, uses ginger, saffron, cinnamon, cloves and pepper throughout.[^4] Christmas pudding's spices, nutmeg, cinnamon, cloves, allspice, also represent this tradition.

However, they were very expensive due to the insane supply chain. Pepper from India's Malabar Coast, cloves and nutmeg from the Maluku Islands in modern Indonesia, passing through something like twelve intermediaries, local producers, Indian Ocean traders, Arab merchants, and finally Venetian buyers who held near-exclusive European purchasing rights.[^5] A pound of pepper in 14th-century England cost a week's wages for a skilled labourer.[^6]

This is partly what drove colonial expansion. The East India Company was chartered on 31 December 1600 specifically to break into the spice trade and cut out the middlemen.[^7] The whole project was, at least initially, about securing cheaper access to the flavours that English cooking already used.

## so where did the spices go

Once the Portuguese and Dutch established direct trade routes, prices fell, and when spices stopped being expensive they stopped being status symbols. The upper classes dropped them. By the late 16th and 17th centuries, new culinary philosophies were emphasising the "natural" flavours of ingredients over heavy spicing.[^8]

France built a replacement system. La Varenne's *Le Cuisinier François* in 1651 is effectively the founding document of modern French cooking, moving explicitly away from medieval spice-heavy sauces toward butter, herbs and stock.[^10] This is where the French cuisine we recognise starts. It was a deliberate pivot from one flavour system to another. Britain didn't do that, other than the rich, who did just copy the French.[^11]

The Reformation made the gap worse as whilst Puritanism didn't produce a *specific* ban on tasty food, it created a cultural atmosphere suspicious of sensory indulgence in general. William Prynne attacked feasting alongside theatre as ungodly excess in 1633.[^12] English cooking became deliberately austere after the Reformation, in contrast to its elaborate medieval predecessor.[^13] If taking pleasure in food is bordserline sinful, you're probably not going to invest much effort in developing a replacement for the spices you just lost.

This wasn't uniquely British, however. German and Dutch food get characterised as bland in a similar way, heavy, starch-forward, sausage-and-potato. The same confluence of forces, Protestantism, industrialisation, the loss of a spice-using tradition without a distinctive replacement, produced similar outcomes across Northern Europe.

## then industrialisation locked it in

By the time the factories arrived, the food infrastructure had already been hollowed out. The enclosure movements of the 18th and early 19th centuries had stripped rural labourers of common land, which meant losing the domestic food economy that went with it: home-brewed small beer, kept pigs, garden vegetables.[^14] This meant the factory worker hadn't just had their time compressed, they'd also had their whole food supply taken away and replaced with whatever could be bought cheaply.

The Factory Act 1833 was the first effective legislation restricting working hours and mandating meal breaks in textile mills.[^15] Before this, factory workers ate when they could, often snatching food between shifts in appalling conditions. The Mines Act of 1842 documented the nutrition of workers underground and it wasn't good. Subsequent Factory Acts in 1844, 1847 and 1850 progressively regulated break times across industries, but the breaks were short. If you've got thirty minutes to eat before your next shift, the food has to be quick, portable and calorie-dense.

This is where the food we think of as "traditionally British" actually comes from. Fish and chips emerged in the 1860s, cheap trawled cod and fried potatoes eaten from newspaper with your hands.[^16] Pie and mash shops followed in the 1870s. The Cornish pasty became the definitive miners' lunch, a complete meal sealed in a thick pastry case that kept it warm for hours underground. The crimped edge supposedly served as a handle so tin miners with arsenic on their hands could hold it without contaminating the filling, then throw the crust away.[^17] Some pasties even had a savoury filling at one end and fruit at the other, comprising a two-course meal you could eat with one hand.

Even the drinks tell the same story. In the 1820s, a radical journalist called William Cobbett railed against the spread of tea among working people, arguing it was making them weak and pale compared to their beer-drinking forebears.[^18] He had a point, in a narrow sense. A pint of small beer provided 150 to 200 calories plus B vitamins from the yeast, while tea provided nothing unless you could afford milk and sugar. Workers had traditionally received beer rations as part of their wages. Even the national drink was, for a time, evaluated primarily on whether it could keep a labourer standing.

Food rationing ran from 1940 to 1954, fourteen years that hit exactly when British cooking might have started recovering.[^19] A generation of cooks grew up without access to ingredients. The skills and traditions that might have survived the industrial period had one more layer stripped away. By the time rationing ended, there wasn't much left to recover.

## none of this is inherent

Heavy and bland doesn't always mean bad. A good pork pie is a good pork pie. Fish and chips done well is great. You just probably don't want to eat these exact dishes every single day, and the reason so much British food looks like this isn't because British people don't care about flavour. It's because a specific sequence of forces played out over about five hundred years: the spices got cheap so the rich stopped using them, sugar stayed expensive so the sweetness stayed, nobody built a replacement cuisine for ordinary people, Puritanism made plainness a virtue, enclosure stripped the rural food economy, industrialisation made speed a necessity, and rationing knocked out whatever was left.

Anyway, I'm still at the pub, and the Marmite pickled egg is nearly gone. This egg isn't bland at all. It's deeply, aggressively flavoured in a way that would probably (and does) horrify anyone who isn't already committed to it. It's not bland, it's just an acquired taste: brown sauce, Marmite, real ale, extremely aged cheese, jellied eels if you're brave. A cuisine that rewards commitment rather than seducing on first contact. Which is, I think, a more honest description of what's going on.
