My nan makes something she calls waffle berry pudding. It's pre-made toasting waffles, raspberries, chocolate and Birds custard, assembled into a baked pudding. It's incredibly sweet and everything in it comes from a supermarket. It's also one of the meals I think about most.

I've been reading Pete Brown on spaghetti bolognese as a British dish[^4], and it got me thinking about what British food actually is for ordinary people right now. Not the heritage canon of roasts and pies and puddings, not the restaurant tier, not what food media tells you that you should be cooking. The actual weeknight rotation that most households cycle through without really thinking about it.

## the weeknight canon

Most British households rotate through maybe ten to fifteen dishes during the week. Spaghetti bolognese, tikka masala, lasagne, a stir fry, fajitas, something with cous cous, shepherd's pie, pesto pasta. Almost all of them are borrowed from somewhere else, and almost all of them have mutated so far from their source that they're functionally different dishes.

British spag bol has diverged so far from ragù alla bolognese that calling them the same thing is generous. No cream, no milk, no pancetta. Lean mince, tinned tomatoes, dried herbs, maybe a stock cube, served over spaghetti rather than tagliatelle. These dishes, despite nominally spanning Italy, India, China, North Africa and Mexico, all end up looking similar in British kitchens. They're what's easy to cook on a weeknight for a family without much fuss, and that convergence is probably the most accurate description of what British people actually eat.

Part of why the weeknight canon looks like this is that the traditional British food canon is heavy. Roasts, pies, fish and chips, steak and kidney pudding. If you eat that every night you feel lethargic. It's also not obvious what traditional British food you eat in summer. The borrowed dishes are lighter, more varied, and more sustainable as everyday meals. A bowl of pasta or a stir fry on a Tuesday just works in a way that a pie doesn't.

The supermarket had a lot to do with this. The jar sauce aisle, e.g., Dolmio, Patak's, Blue Dragon, is basically a map of British weeknight cooking. Each jar represents a cuisine collapsed into a single flavour profile, and the sequence in which they appeared on shelves roughly tracks the sequence in which those dishes entered the rotation. The supply chain shaped the repertoire as much as migration or travel did.

This is fairly unique to Britain. French weeknight cooking is still overwhelmingly French, e.g., steak-frites, quiche, omelettes, gratin. German households often eat Abendbrot, a cold meal of bread, cheese and cold cuts. Spanish and Italian home cooking is almost entirely domestic. The Netherlands is the closest parallel, with Indonesian food genuinely adopted from colonial history, but the range is narrower. According to Euromonitor data, the UK ready meals market is roughly double France's and triple Germany's, and a typical large British supermarket stocks over 150 varieties of international cooking sauces compared to maybe a third of that in a French equivalent.[^2] Britain didn't just borrow more dishes, it built an entire supermarket infrastructure around them.

## the embarrassment

People are slightly ashamed of this. There's a background awareness that your spag bol isn't "authentic," that your stir fry doesn't have wok hei, that your fajitas aren't what anyone in Mexico would recognise. That awareness is culturally specific. French home cooks don't feel sheepish about making a gratin.

Some of it comes from food media. [Thomas Straker's](https://www.instagram.com/thomasstraker/) philosophy is essentially that if you just use the best olive oil and the best tomatoes, everything tastes delicious. Which is true, but a kilo of peak-season heritage tomatoes costs something like £20-25 and a decent bottle of extra virgin is £30+. What's being demonstrated is purchasing power, not cooking skill, and it has nothing to do with how most people actually cook. [Julius Roberts](https://www.instagram.com/juliusroberts/) films himself cooking from his Dorset garden with hand-thrown ceramics and foraged ingredients in golden-hour lighting. Lovely to watch, but it requires land, a rural property, and the financial freedom to leave a salaried career. I've [written before](/blog/posts/why-language-models-cant-cook/) about how food media often optimises for selling a lifestyle rather than building genuine understanding, and I think the same dynamic applies here.

In *The Authenticity Hoax*, Andrew Potter describes this broader pattern as the fetishization of the pre-modern past and exotic cultures, and a preference for increasingly rarefied forms of consumption over the perceived shallowness of mainstream Western life.[^1] I think that framing applies, but the specific version in British food culture is more pointed. It's not anti-British in some general sense, it's dismissive of what people actually eat. The weeknight canon, which is the actual national cuisine, gets treated as something to graduate from rather than something to engage with seriously.

You can see this in how restaurants handle it. Places like [St. John](https://stjohnrestaurant.com/) and [Higher Ground](https://www.highergroundmcr.co.uk/) do serve British food, but it's a postmodern, heritage-inflected version of it, e.g., devilled tripe, roasted bone marrow, spleen. It's an illustration of what British food could be through a historical lens, and it's good, but nobody eats that at home. On the other side, restaurants like [Towpath](https://www.towpathcafe.com/), [Mountain](https://www.mountainlondon.co.uk/) and [Brat](https://www.bratrestaurant.co.uk/) use excellent British produce but cook what is basically modern European food, French and Spanish and Italian techniques with British ingredients. Both are worth celebrating, but neither is championing the food that people actually cook on a Tuesday night.

The contrast with other countries is noticeable. A bistro in Paris, a taverna in Greece, a tapas bar in Spain, these are humble, homely places that serve the food people actually eat, done well, and take pride in it. Britain doesn't really have an equivalent. If someone opened a café that served a really good jacket potato, a really good cheese sandwich, a really good lasagne, a really good chilli con carne, just the weeknight canon done properly and with care, I think people would love it. But what we have is either the high-end restaurant interpretation or the cheap café where the roast beef tastes terrible. The middle ground, where you just champion what people eat and make it well, barely exists.

Ruby Tandoh has pointed out how weirdly performative a lot of online food content is, optimised for social media engagement rather than for actually being cooked again.[^5] I think she's right, and the distinction matters. The dishes that persist are the ones people make on a Tuesday without thinking about it, not the ones that get millions of views and disappear. The weeknight canon is defined by repetition, not spectacle.

## depth comes from the person, not the dish

The standard critique of British weeknight cooking is that it's "thin," that it lacks the depth of food cultures with longer roots and more regional variation. I don't think that's true, or at least I think it misidentifies where depth actually comes from.

My nan's waffle berry pudding is entirely constructed from industrial products. But the fact that she makes it, her particular way of assembling it, the specific times and people it's associated with, that gives it genuine cultural weight. The mechanism is the same as an Italian grandmother's ragù. The ragù has a longer historical pedigree and more "legitimate" ingredients, and that heritage does add something. But the majority of what makes it meaningful to the person eating it is that a specific person made it, repeatedly, for you.

I don't think you engage much more deeply with food just because it's been in your family for longer. People like to think they do, but most of that connection comes from its association with a living person who provides it. Strip away the person and you're left with a recipe, which is just information.

This means that British supermarket-assembled food is exactly as culturally deep as any other food culture, provided it's being made by someone for someone. My nan's waffle berry pudding has the same cultural density as a cassoulet that's been in a French family for four generations. The cassoulet just has better PR.

## making it well, on its own terms

The other thing that gets missed is you can make these borrowed dishes well without pretending they're something they're not.

English-style fajitas, for example. You don't have to use pre-made seasoning packets. You can just use your own spices and it's actually really nice. It's not Mexican food, and that's fine because we don't have Mexican ingredients and it's not really what British people are after anyway. It's its own thing. A stir fry in a pan at home is a bit different from what you'd get from a wok at proper heat, but it can be good on its own terms. Spag bol made with care and decent ingredients is a satisfying meal regardless of what they think in Bologna.

We don't choose what's available in the supermarkets and we don't always have the budget for an organic roast chicken. That's fine. Work with what's there, make it well, and stop apologising for it.

## a cuisine mid-selection

I think what you're looking at when you look at British weeknight cooking is a food culture in its early formation rather than one that's absent or thin. The raw material is internationally sourced and supermarket-mediated, the depth is relational, and the filter for what persists is whether it's good enough to make again.

Part of why it looks like this is that Britain has genuinely poor food culture infrastructure. In the UK, food policy sits in DEFRA, which treats it as agriculture and supply chain safety. It doesn't sit in DCMS, which handles culture. There is no equivalent to France's UNESCO inscription for its gastronomic meal tradition in 2010, or Italy's legal protections for regional food products, or the University of Gastronomic Sciences in Piedmont. The UK has never even submitted a food-related nomination to UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list. When Eric Pickles proposed "curry colleges" to train British-born curry chefs in 2012, the idea was widely mocked.[^3] The one visible attempt at food culture policy became a punchline.

This is why supermarkets ended up as the architects. Not by cultural design, but by default. The profit margins on jar sauces shaped the national repertoire because nothing else was going to. That's not unique to Britain, all cuisines are shaped by material and economic forces, but most other countries also have institutional support that runs alongside the market. Britain basically just has the market.

But people are already making it their own. Worcestershire sauce in spag bol, Marmite or Bovril for depth, brown sauce on the side, cocoa powder in chilli con carne. These are small, personal adjustments that turn a borrowed dish into something specific. That's the same mechanism that produces regional variation in any cuisine, it's just happening informally and without institutional recognition.

Some of the British weeknight canon will persist. Spag bol almost certainly will because it's cheap, flexible, and satisfying. Some of it won't. What lasts will be what's genuinely good enough, and over time those dishes will accumulate the kind of history and variation that other cultures call "traditional."

I'll probably make something like my nan's waffle berry pudding for my own children. A bit less sweet, a bit less mushy. And that small adjustment, repeated across generations, is the same mechanism that produces the dishes other cultures are proud of. It's just younger here.

British food is what British people eat. It's not that complicated.
