My nan makes something she calls waffle berry pudding. It's pre-made toasting waffles, raspberries, chocolate and Birds custard, assembled into a baked pudding. It's incredibly sweet and everything in it comes from a supermarket. It's also one of the meals I think about most.

I've been reading Pete Brown on spaghetti bolognese as a British dish, and it got me thinking about what British food actually is for ordinary people right now. Not the heritage canon of roasts and pies and puddings, not the restaurant tier, not what food media tells you that you should be cooking. The actual weeknight rotation that most households cycle through without really thinking about it.

## the weeknight canon

Most British households rotate through maybe ten to fifteen dishes during the week. Spaghetti bolognese, tikka masala, lasagne, a stir fry, fajitas, something with cous cous, shepherd's pie, pesto pasta. Almost all of them are borrowed from somewhere else, and almost all of them have mutated so far from their source that they're functionally different dishes.

British spag bol has diverged so far from ragù alla bolognese that calling them the same thing is generous. No cream, no milk, no pancetta. Lean mince, tinned tomatoes, dried herbs, maybe a stock cube, served over spaghetti rather than tagliatelle. The point is that these dishes, despite nominally spanning Italy, India, China, North Africa and Mexico, all end up structurally similar in British kitchens. Protein, a carb, and a flavouring agent from a jar or a tin. That convergence is the cuisine. The shared template, repeated across a dozen borrowed dishes, is probably the most accurate description of what British people actually eat.

The supermarket had a lot to do with this. The jar sauce aisle, e.g., Dolmio, Patak's, Blue Dragon, is basically a map of British weeknight cooking. Each jar represents a cuisine collapsed into a single flavour profile, and the sequence in which they appeared on shelves roughly tracks the sequence in which those dishes entered the rotation. The supply chain shaped the repertoire as much as migration or travel did.

## the embarrassment

People are slightly ashamed of this. There's a background awareness that your spag bol isn't "authentic," that your stir fry doesn't have wok hei, that your fajitas aren't what anyone in Mexico would recognise. That awareness is culturally specific. French home cooks don't feel sheepish about making a gratin.

I think some of it comes from food media. There's a long tradition of selling people the idea that they should be eating differently, more authentically, more connected to somewhere that isn't where they actually live. I've [written before](/blog/posts/why-language-models-cant-cook/) about how celebrity chefs often optimise for selling products and brand extensions rather than building genuine understanding, and I think the same dynamic applies more broadly. A lot of food content, especially online, trades on a fantasy of continental living, the idea that you could be having a vermut with some boquerones somewhere warm when actually you're in Bethnal Green and it's raining.

In *The Authenticity Hoax*, Andrew Potter describes this pattern as "the fetishization of our pre-modern past and its contemporary incarnation in exotic cultures, the search for increasingly obscure and rarefied forms of consumption and experience... and most obviously, an almost violent hostility to the perceived shallowness of Western forms of consumption and entertainment." That was written in 2009. I think it's intensified since then, just shifted from cookbooks to social media, where food creators film themselves cooking in rural kitchens with hand-thrown ceramics and foraged ingredients. Lovely to watch, but nothing to do with how most people eat or could eat.

There's a distinction worth making here between the persistent weeknight canon and the transitory stuff. Viral recipe content that's optimised for social media engagement rather than for actually being cooked again is a different phenomenon entirely. A food writer I like has pointed out how weirdly performative a lot of that content is, and I think she's right. The dishes that matter are the ones people make on a Tuesday without thinking about it, not the ones that get millions of views and disappear.

## depth comes from the person, not the dish

The standard critique of British weeknight cooking is that it's "thin," that it lacks the depth of food cultures with longer roots and more regional variation. I don't think that's true, or at least I think it misidentifies where depth actually comes from.

My nan's waffle berry pudding is entirely constructed from industrial products. But the fact that she makes it, her particular way of assembling it, the specific times and people it's associated with, that gives it genuine cultural weight. The mechanism is the same as an Italian grandmother's ragù. The ragù has a longer historical pedigree and more "legitimate" ingredients, but that's not what makes it meaningful to the person eating it. What makes it meaningful is that a specific person made it, repeatedly, for you.

I don't think you engage more deeply with food just because it's been in your family for longer. People like to think they do, but the majority of that connection comes from its association with a living person who provides it. Strip away the person and you're left with a recipe, which is just information.

This means that British supermarket-assembled food is exactly as culturally deep as any other food culture, provided it's being made by someone for someone. My nan's waffle berry pudding has the same cultural density as a cassoulet that's been in a French family for four generations. The cassoulet just has better PR.

## making it well, on its own terms

The other thing that gets missed is you can make these borrowed dishes well without pretending they're something they're not.

English-style fajitas, for example. You don't have to use pre-made seasoning packets. You can just use your own spices and it's actually really nice. It's not Mexican food, and that's fine because we don't have Mexican ingredients and it's not really what British people are after anyway. It's its own thing. A stir fry in a pan at home is a bit different from what you'd get from a wok at proper heat, but it can be good on its own terms. Spag bol made with care and decent ingredients is a satisfying meal regardless of what they think in Bologna.

We don't choose what's available in the supermarkets and we don't always have the budget for an organic roast chicken. That's fine. Work with what's there, make it well, and stop apologising for it.

## a cuisine mid-selection

I think what you're looking at when you look at British weeknight cooking is a food culture in its early formation rather than one that's absent or thin. The raw material is internationally sourced and supermarket-mediated, the depth is relational, and the filter for what persists is whether it's good enough to make again.

That's not a lesser process than any other. Ragù alla bolognese didn't survive because Italy has superior food culture infrastructure. It survived because it's a good dish that rewards repetition and tolerates variation. Plenty of Italian home dishes didn't make it. We just don't hear about them because they're gone.

Some of the British weeknight canon will persist. Spag bol almost certainly will because it's cheap, flexible, and satisfying. Some of it won't. What lasts will be what's genuinely good enough, and over time those dishes will accumulate the kind of history and variation that other cultures call "traditional."

I'll probably make something like my nan's waffle berry pudding for my own children. A bit less sweet, a bit less mushy. And that small adjustment, repeated across generations, is the same mechanism that produces the dishes other cultures are proud of. It's just younger here.

British food is what British people eat. It's not that complicated.
