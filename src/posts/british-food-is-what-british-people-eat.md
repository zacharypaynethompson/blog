My nan makes something she calls waffle berry pudding, which consists of pre-made toasting waffles, raspberries, chocolate and Bird's custard, assembled into a baked pudding. It's incredibly sweet and everything in it comes from a supermarket. It's also one of the few meals I can say comes from a "family recipe".

I've been reading a book by Pete Brown called "In Defence of British Food" and in it he highlights spaghetti bolognese as a British dish[^4], and it got me thinking about what British food actually is for ordinary people right now. Not the heritage canon of roasts and pies and puddings, not the restaurant tier, not what food media tells you that you should be cooking. The actual weeknight rotation that most households cycle through without really thinking about it.

## the weeknight rotation

Most British households rotate through maybe ten to fifteen dishes during the week. Perhaps that's spaghetti bolognese, tikka masala, lasagne, a stir fry, fajitas, something with... cous cous(?), shepherd's pie, pesto pasta. Almost all of them are borrowed from somewhere else, and almost all of them have mutated so far from their source that they're functionally different dishes.

British spag bol has diverged so far from ragù alla bolognese that calling them the same thing is quite generous: no cream, no milk, no pancetta, just lean mince, tinned tomatoes, dried herbs, maybe a stock cube, served over spaghetti rather than tagliatelle. These dishes, despite nominally spanning Italy, India, China, North Africa and Mexico, all end up looking rather similar in British kitchens. They're what's easy to cook on a weeknight for a family without much fuss, and that convergence is probably the most accurate description of what British people actually eat.

Part of why the weeknight rotation looks like this is that the traditional British food canon is heavy. Roasts, pies, fish and chips, steak and kidney pudding. If you eat that every night, and you work a desk job, you're gonna feel... lethargic. It's also not obvious to me what "traditional" British food you eat in summer. The borrowed dishes are lighter, more varied, and more sustainable as everyday meals. A bowl of pasta or a stir fry on a Tuesday just works in a way that a pie doesn't.

The supermarket had a lot to do with this. The jar sauce aisle, e.g., Dolmio, Patak's, Blue Dragon, is a map of British weeknight cooking. Each jar represents a cuisine collapsed into a handful of flavour profiles, and the sequence in which they appeared on shelves roughly tracks the sequence in which those dishes entered the rotation. The supply chain shaped the repertoire as much as migration or travel did.

This is fairly unique to Britain. French weeknight cooking is still overwhelmingly French, e.g., steak-frites, quiche, omelettes, gratin. German households often eat something called Abendbrot, a cold meal of bread, cheese and cold cuts. Spanish and Italian home cooking is almost entirely domestic. The Netherlands is the closest parallel, with Indonesian food genuinely adopted from colonial history, but the range is narrower. According to Euromonitor data, the UK ready meals market is roughly double France's and triple Germany's, and a typical large British supermarket stocks over 150 varieties of international cooking sauces compared to maybe a third of that in a French equivalent.[^2] Britain didn't just borrow more dishes, it built an entire supermarket infrastructure around them.

## the embarrassment

I think most people are slightly ashamed of this. There's a background awareness that your spag bol isn't "authentic," that your stir fry doesn't have wok hei, that your fajitas aren't what anyone in Mexico would recognise. That awareness is culturally specific. Do French home cooks feel sheepish about making a gratin?

Some of it comes from food media. [Thomas Straker's](https://thomasstraker.com/home/) philosophy is essentially that if you just use the best olive oil and the best tomatoes, everything tastes delicious. Which is obviously true, but a kilo of peak-season heritage tomatoes costs something like £20-25 and a decent bottle of extra virgin is £30+. What's mostly being demonstrated is purchasing power, not cooking skill, and it has nothing to do with how most people actually cook. [Julius Roberts](https://www.juliusroberts.co.uk/) films himself cooking from his Dorset garden with hand-thrown ceramics and foraged ingredients in golden-hour lighting. Lovely to watch, but it requires land, a rural property, and the financial freedom to leave a salaried career. I've [written before](/blog/posts/why-language-models-cant-cook/) about how food media often optimises for selling a lifestyle rather than building genuine understanding, and the same dynamic applies here.

In *The Authenticity Hoax*, Andrew Potter describes this broader pattern as the fetishization of the pre-modern past and exotic cultures, and a preference for increasingly rarefied forms of consumption over the perceived shallowness of mainstream Western life.[^1] That framing applies, but the specific version in British food culture is more pointed. It's not anti-British in some general sense. It's dismissive of what people actually eat. The weeknight rotation, which is the actual national cuisine, gets treated as something to graduate *from* rather than something to engage with seriously.

You can see this in how restaurants handle it. Places like [St. John](https://stjohnrestaurant.com/) and [Higher Ground](https://www.highergroundmcr.co.uk/) do serve British food, but it's a postmodern, heritage-inflected version of it, e.g., devilled tripe, roasted bone marrow... spleen. It's an illustration of what British food could be through a historical lens, and it is good, but nobody eats that at home. On the other side, restaurants like [Towpath](https://www.towpathlondon.com/), [Mountain](https://mountainbeakstreet.com/) and [Brat](https://bratrestaurant.co.uk/climpsons-arch/) (sometimes) use excellent British produce but cook what is basically modern European food, French and Spanish and Italian techniques with British ingredients. Both are worth celebrating, but neither is championing the food that people actually cook on that Tuesday night.

This is more salient when you compare to how this looks in peer countries. A bistro in Paris, a taverna in Greece, a tapas bar in Spain, these are humble, homely places that serve the food people actually eat, done well, and take pride in it. Britain doesn't really have an equivalent. If someone opened a café that served a *really* good jacket potato, a *really* good cheese sandwich, a *really* good lasagne, a *really* good chilli con carne, just the weeknight rotation done properly and with care, I think people would love it. But what we have is either the high-end restaurant interpretation or the cheap café where the breakfasts are nice, but the roast beef tastes like farts and brings back all the worst memories of a Thursday school dinner. The middle ground, where you just champion what people eat and make it well, doesn't really exist.

Ruby Tandoh has pointed out how weirdly performative a lot of online food content is, optimised for social media engagement rather than for actually being cooked again.[^5] She's right, and that distinction is important. The dishes that persist are the ones people make on that Tuesday without thinking about it, not the ones that get millions of views and disappear. The weeknight rotation is defined by repetition, not spectacle.

## depth comes from the person, not the dish

One critique of British weeknight cooking could be that it's "thin," that it lacks the depth of food cultures with longer roots and more regional variation. I don't think that's true, or at least it misidentifies where depth actually comes from.

My nan's waffle berry pudding is entirely constructed from industrial products. But the fact that she makes it, her particular way of assembling it, the specific times and people it's associated with, that gives it genuine cultural weight. The mechanism is the same as an Italian grandmother's ragù. The ragù has a longer historical pedigree and more "legitimate" ingredients, and that heritage does add something. But the majority of what makes it meaningful to the person eating it is that a specific person made it, repeatedly, for you.

I don't think you engage much more deeply with food just because it's been in your family for longer. People like to think they do, but most of that connection comes from its association with a living person who provides it. Strip away the person and you're left with a recipe, which is just information.

This means that British supermarket-assembled food is as culturally deep as any other food culture, provided it's being made by someone for someone. My nan's waffle berry pudding has the same cultural density as a cassoulet that's been in a French family for four generations. The cassoulet just has better PR, and a fancy dish, and, admittedly, does taste much better.

## making it well, on its own terms

The other thing that gets missed is you can make these borrowed dishes well without pretending they're something they're not.

"English-style" fajitas, for example. You don't *have* to use pre-made seasoning packets. You can just use freshly ground spices and it's actually very nice. It's not Mexican food, and that's fine because we don't have Mexican ingredients and it's not really what British people are after anyway. It's its own thing. A stir fry in a pan at home is quite a bit different from what you'd get from a wok at proper heat, but it *can* be good on its own terms. Spag bol made with care and decent ingredients is a satisfying meal regardless of what they think in Bologna.

We don't choose what's available in the supermarkets and we don't always have the budget for an organic roast chicken or Natoora tomatoes. That's fine. Work with what's there, make it well, and we can stop apologising for it.

## a cuisine mid-selection

What you're possibly looking at when you look at British weeknight cooking is a food culture in its early formation rather than one that's absent or thin. The raw material is internationally sourced and supermarket-mediated, the depth is relational, and the filter for what persists is whether it's good enough to make again.

Part of why it looks like this is that Britain has genuinely poor food culture infrastructure. In the UK, food policy sits in DEFRA (the Department for the Environment, Food and Rural Affairs), which treats it as agriculture and supply chain safety. It doesn't sit in DCMS (the Department for Culture, Media and Sport), which handles culture. There is no equivalent to France's UNESCO inscription for its gastronomic meal tradition in 2010, or Italy's legal protections for regional food products, or the University of Gastronomic Sciences in Piedmont. The UK has never submitted any nomination to the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list, because it only ratified the underlying convention in 2024 and has explicitly deferred nominations.

France had an appellation system for wine dating to 1905.[^6] The UK never developed anything equivalent. It just adopted EU geographical indication schemes wholesale and continued them post-Brexit. British Food Fortnight has run since 2002, but it's entirely funded by private sponsors rather than government.[^7] When British cheese nearly disappeared under wartime rationing, the recovery took forty years and was driven entirely by private efforts like Neal's Yard Dairy, not policy.[^8] Today, Slow Food UK catalogues endangered British foods through its Ark of Taste programme, heritage preservation work that in France or Italy would be at least partly government-supported.[^9] Henry Dimbleby's National Food Strategy in 2021 made serious recommendations for food culture and education; key proposals were rejected.[^10] When Eric Pickles proposed "curry colleges" to train British-born curry chefs in 2012, the idea was widely mocked.[^3] The pattern is consistent. The few attempts at food culture policy either get no funding or become punchlines.

This is even more clear when you contrast this with other countries. Thailand's Global Thai programme in 2002 aimed to spread Thai restaurants worldwide with government-backed loans of up to $3 million for restaurateurs.[^11] Korea invested $77 million in hansik globalisation in 2009.[^12] Japan secured UNESCO inscription for washoku in 2013 as a deliberate soft power strategy.[^13] These are countries that recognise food as cultural infrastructure worth investing in. Britain doesn't, and the weeknight rotation is partly what you get instead. I want to write more about this in the future, what food policy could look like if we took our actual food culture more seriously.

There *is* contemporary parliamentary interest in this, though it hasn't translated into much. In the Hospitality Sector debate in September 2025, MPs across parties described pubs and restaurants as the "heart" of communities, linking them to tourism, local identity and social cohesion.[^14] Written questions to DEFRA in early 2026 used surprisingly direct language, with ministers saying the food strategy should "champion UK food cultures and celebrate British food" and support food that is "uniquely British."[^15] Richard Holden made the comparison explicit in a 2024 debate on pie and mash: France has over 800 protected food products, Italy just under that, the UK has fewer than 100. He argued Britain should do more to talk up foods for their place in the country's "heritage, national cuisine and national heart."[^17] But even then, the structural question goes unasked. Nobody has ever argued in Parliament that food should be treated as culture rather than agriculture, or that policy should sit with DCMS rather than DEFRA.

The government's ambition is for half of public sector food to be locally produced or certified to higher standards.[^16] There's a £10 million hospitality support fund, business rates relief for pubs and live music venues, and planned reforms to licensing and planning.[^18] But this is still food policy filtered through supply chains and procurement. When home cooking appears in Parliament at all, it's through a public health lens, e.g., MPs discussing obesity and convenience food, parents struggling to "prepare wholesome meals from scratch," the temptation of packet sauces.[^19] It's framed as a problem to solve, not a culture to engage with. Nobody in Parliament has ever mentioned our weeknight meals, the spag bol, the curry, the stir fry, the fajitas, as something worth taking seriously in its own right. The closest anyone has got to a food-related UNESCO bid is a written question about whether cask ale could be nominated (thank you).[^20] Multiple MPs have explicitly criticised the gap between rhetoric and action, e.g., "warm words" that aren't "backed up with sound policymaking," debates that end with members "a little frustrated that we have not quite got the answers we wanted."[^21]

Pubs could theoretically be the humble middle-ground venue I mentioned earlier, the equivalent of a French bistro or Spanish tapas bar, a place that serves the food people actually eat, done well, with pride. And in some parts of the country they are. Independent pubs in Sheffield, Leeds, Liverpool often do serve decent, homely food at reasonable prices. But in London especially, the pub landscape is dominated by chains, e.g., Greene King and Fullers, and the food tends to be overpriced, reheated and terrible. Ten pounds for a dry Scotch egg is too much for me even after four pints of bitter. Parliament rarely discusses pub food quality itself. When pubs come up, it's about rates, closures, alcohol duty, community role, not what's actually on the plate.[^22] Julie Minns pointed out in a 2024 debate that many "free houses are not free at all" because supply contracts with major brewers restrict what they can serve.[^23] The venue that could be championing everyday British food mostly isn't, though there are exceptions worth investigating.

Supermarkets ended up as the architects of our weeknight meal rotation. Not by cultural design, but by default. The profit margins on jar sauces and packet spice mixes shaped the national repertoire because nothing else was going to. That's not unique to Britain, all cuisines are shaped by material and economic forces, but most other countries also have institutional support running alongside the market. Britain basically just has the market.

## making it our own

But people are already making it their own. Think about that spag bol recipe. For me it's infused with visions of my Mum putting in "secret" touches, like Worcestershire sauce, Marmite and even brown sauce. To some that might sound a little kooky, but these are the small, personal adjustments that turn a borrowed dish into something specific. That's the same mechanism that produces regional variation in any cuisine, it's just happening informally and without institutional recognition.

Some of the British weeknight rotation will persist. Spag bol almost certainly will because it's cheap, flexible, and satisfying. Some of it won't. What lasts will be what's genuinely good enough, and over time those dishes will accumulate the kind of history and variation that other cultures currently call "traditional."

I'll probably make something *like* my nan's waffle berry pudding for my own family. Perhaps a bit less sweet, maybe a bit less mushy. Those small adjustments, repeated across generations, are the same mechanism that produces the dishes other cultures are proud of. It's just younger here.

## and that's fine

Authenticity, to some extent, *is* a hoax. We decide where the depth comes from. It comes from the person who makes the food for you or who you make it for, not only from the dish's historical pedigree or the legitimacy of its ingredients. You can make those weeknight meals well, with care and decent ingredients, without needing a Dorset smallholding or heritage tomatoes. And those constraints we're under, the poor food culture infrastructure, the supermarket-shaped repertoire, the absence of institutional support, none of that is our fault. We can make do with what we've got.

British food is what British people eat.
